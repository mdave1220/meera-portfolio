THE PROBLEM //

Late elementary and middle school is when peer relationships shift fastest, yet most SEL curricula still rely on passive videos, worksheets, and guest speakers that rarely change behavior. Teasing and exclusion stay normalized ("it's just jokes… until it's not"), and conflict often starts in group chats adults never see. Students can define empathy; the failure mode is acting on it under real social pressure.

DISCOVERY //

Across 15 interviews with students, parents, and teachers, the same patterns kept surfacing: social fear, format preferences, and hard privacy lines. A few moments from the sessions:

DESIGN DIRECTION //

We mapped three divergent concepts: empathy coaching (building the words to speak up), peer norming (showing others would act too), and a game-driven identity system. Interviews showed no single direction solved both barriers. Coaching alone left social fear intact; norming alone left students without language to use. We combined coaching and anonymized peer data, grounded in experiential learning and social norming research, for ages 11–13.

THE PRODUCT //

Kindly places students in realistic moments: being left out of a group chat, witnessing teasing at lunch, navigating a rumor that pits loyalty against truth. It asks them to decide what to do before seeing the outcome. Immediate emotional feedback and response coaching follow each choice; anonymized peer stats then show how classmates actually responded, correcting the assumption that everyone else would stay silent.

ITERATION //

Early concepts tested whether students could identify empathetic responses. They already could. We reframed around decision-making under peer influence. Class testing and interview feedback pushed the build toward instant consequence feedback (not end-of-scenario summaries), scaffolded difficulty tiers, and a progression loop with empathy points, streaks, and a leaderboard to keep sessions under five minutes.

DESIGN CONSTRAINTS //

Tools that auto-escalate to teachers would backfire. Students won't report if they fear social backlash or being labeled dramatic. We designed for private practice with transparent, safety-only alert thresholds. Teachers also flagged adoption friction: "We don't need another program unless it saves time." Kindly had to work without facilitator overhead.

WHAT I OWNED //

Conducted and synthesized learner interviews, wrote the background research and user-findings sections of our proposal and poster, contributed design rationale for our pitch, and co-built the interactive prototype. Also drafted key portions of our final report on how the design pivoted from empathy awareness to situated practice.
