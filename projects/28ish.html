THE BRIEF //

A two-semester, industry-sponsored capstone with a real startup founder. Her mission: a world where women feel confident and in control of their bodies. 28ish is a menstrual health app built to do more than log dates. It helps users understand how each cycle phase affects mood, energy, productivity, and social life. Existing trackers stop at data entry; closing that gap is both a product opportunity and a health equity problem.

THE PROBLEM //

Most period apps do the bare minimum: calendars, symptom logs, and vague predictions. In our research, women said they wanted to understand their cycle, not just track it.

“I never really learned what the phases of my cycle actually mean for me.”
“I wish I knew how to work with my cycle instead of against it.”

Competitors like Flo, Clue, Apple Health, and Glow collect plenty of data but rarely turn it into personalized, actionable insight. Users get dots on a calendar, not context for why energy dips in one phase and focus peaks in another.

RESEARCH //

We ran an anonymous survey with 60+ responses from women 18–34, mixing free response, scales, and multiple choice. That blend of qualitative and quantitative data pinned our target user and surfaced three consistent themes:

Most users felt under-educated about how cycle phases affect daily life. They wanted practical insights tied to energy, focus, and mood, not more logging fields. And every major competitor offered data without enough interpretability or personalization.

DEFINING THE MVP //

Three MVP pillars: cycle tracking, symptom logging, and personalized insights. We scoped a PostgreSQL schema and a 5-sprint Agile plan on Jira. Every sprint demoed to the founder. Cut smartwatch biometrics; deferred AI advising.

Four core flows carried the experience: sign-in / account creation, a gamified onboarding form (sexual health, symptoms, recent period), a daily check-in split into behavioral and menstrual data, and an insights dashboard surfacing mood, work, energy, and sociability by cycle phase, with charts, icons, and short readings instead of walls of text.

WHAT I OWNED //

As product lead, I authored user stories, managed the Jira backlog, and ran weekly client syncs, presenting progress, gathering feedback, and keeping design and engineering aligned on the MVP.

On the build side, I shipped the insights experience end-to-end on React Native/Expo + FastAPI + PostgreSQL: routing, graph library integration, and weekly mood/energy/productivity charts wired to live data. I researched the charting API, mirrored our Figma prototypes in code, and negotiated API contracts so design iterations didn't break data integrity.

ITERATION //

Validated Figma high-fis in Expo Go usability sessions and heuristic evaluations across five scenarios: account creation, onboarding, daily check-in, snoozing a check-in, and reading insights. Period logging needed native calendar affordances; daily check-in needed separate behavioral vs. menstrual form paths with scales and buttons instead of long text fields; symptom data only became useful with drill-down insight categories and color-coded weekly charts.

Onboarding went through several passes to feel gamified without overwhelming users. A central hub let people complete sexual health, symptoms, and recent-period sections in any order, with a progress bar tracking completion. Insights split qualitative readings (especially for social life) from quantitative graphs so users could scan a week at a glance or dive into one category.

TAKEAWAYS //

Weekly founder syncs taught me how much ongoing stakeholder alignment matters in a real product cycle, especially when business priorities and engineering constraints pull in different directions.

Prototyping with a component library in mind (Tamagui) showed me how much smoother the design-dev handoff gets when Figma maps cleanly to code. Owning the roadmap alongside the build pushed me toward product thinking: balancing user needs, client goals, and what we could actually ship in five sprints.
