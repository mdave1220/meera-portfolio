Serve

PRODUCT OWNER & ENGINEER · SOLO · MOBILE APP · PRE-LAUNCH

saving a recipe is easy. actually cooking from it isn't.

  • 15+user interviews

  • 4social primitives scoped

THE BRIEF //

Serve is a social recipe app for people who discover food everywhere: TikTok, Instagram, blogs, group chats. But they lose recipes before they ever cook them. Bookmarks become hoarding; recipe apps demand manual re-entry; notes apps have no structure. The gap isn't finding inspiration, it's closing the loop from "I saw this" to "I'm making this tonight." Serve bets that saving from anywhere, a personal cookbook, and lightweight social proof (what friends actually cook) beats another standalone recipe database.

DISCOVERY //

Ran 15+ semi-structured 1:1 interviews with college students and full-time workers, from new grads learning to cook independently to adults juggling weeknight meals, to understand where recipe apps break down. Top pain points: recipes trapped on social platforms with no easy save path, re-typing ingredients as friction, inspiration that never converts to a grocery run, and a desire to see what people they know actually cook, not just celebrity chefs. That research pinned the core problem: recipe discovery and cooking inspiration, not another logging tool.

DEFINING THE MVP //

Translated interview themes into three launch features: AI voice-to-text recipe input (hands-free, in-kitchen capture), cross-app recipe saving (the core job-to-be-done), and a real-time grocery stock checker (closing inspiration → execution). Social wasn't deferred. I scoped 4 core primitives (follows, likes, comments, DMs) so the product feels alive at launch, not a solo cookbook with sharing bolted on later. Supporting the save flow: an LLM-powered importer that parses unstructured web content into structured recipes, and trigger-driven push notifications on Expo Push API. Deliberately cut meal planning, nutrition tracking, and a web client. Solo owner, pre-launch scope had to complete one honest loop: discover → save → cook → share.

PRE-LAUNCH VALIDATION //

Before writing production code, pressure-tested the concept in Figma and early Expo builds with interview participants. Voice input moved up in priority when users described typing with messy hands mid-recipe; the importer flow was simplified when testers couldn't tell whether a pasted URL had successfully parsed. Each iteration fed back into scope: if a feature didn't survive a real conversation about weeknight cooking, it didn't make the launch cut.

WHAT I OWNED //

End-to-end product ownership from concept through pre-launch build: user interviews and synthesis, MVP definition, launch-feature prioritization, design decisions, and iterative release cadence. On the build side, designed a normalized Supabase Postgres schema to power the social graph, built the LLM importer pipeline, and wired push notifications, staying close enough to implementation that scope calls reflected real technical constraints, not wishful roadmaps.

THE BRIEF //

A two-semester engagement with a real startup founder, weekly client syncs, demo feedback loops, and a written vision statement with problem and positioning against Flo and Period Calendar. Her mission: a world where women feel confident and in control of their bodies, not just informed when their period starts. Women's health is an underserved market, existing trackers stop at data entry: no personalized insights, weak handling of irregular cycles, and zero feedback loop. Users log everything and learn nothing. 28ish exists because closing that gap is both a product opportunity and a health equity problem.

DISCOVERY //

Because the data is sensitive, we deliberately chose an anonymous survey over interviews, 60+ responses pinned our target user (women 18–34, North America) and the top pain points: complexity, lack of personalized insights, and privacy concerns.

DEFINING THE MVP //

I helped translate that research into three MVP pillars: cycle tracking, symptom logging, and personalized insights. User needs collided with data-modeling constraints, insights require structured, relational data over time, so we scoped a PostgreSQL schema (daily check-ins, conditional questions, user-cycle joins) and a 5-sprint plan from user stories: data collection → time-series insights → monthly reports, with health advising as a stretch goal. We deliberately cut smartwatch biometrics and deferred AI advising to post-MVP, every sprint had to demo to a real founder, so scope had to be honest about what the data model could support on day one.

ITERATION //

We developed Figma high-fis and validated them in Expo Go usability sessions after semester-one and sprint-two client demos. Key findings drove the diffs in the artifacts above: period logging needed native calendar affordances (not just styled date tiles); daily check-in couldn't be a single catch-all button, users needed separate behavioral vs. menstrual form paths; and symptom data only became useful once we built drill-down insight categories with color-coded weekly charts.

WHAT I OWNED //

On a React Native/Expo + Firebase Auth stack backed by Python/FastAPI and PostgreSQL, I shipped the insights experience end-to-end, routing, styling, graph library integration, and wiring weekly mood / energy / productivity charts to live data from Naman's FastAPI endpoints, plus a monthly recap feature and team project management. I also sat on the seam between design, engineering, and data: aligning the Figma system with Arvind's Expo architecture and the PostgreSQL EERD so frontend forms mapped cleanly to backend tables, running peer code reviews across the React Native codebase, keeping sprint deliverables and client meeting minutes current, and negotiating API contracts for the insights pages so design iterations didn't break data integrity.

TravelJAM

PRODUCT DESIGNER · TEAM OF 4 · FULL DESIGN LIFECYCLE

can a TikTok-style feed make trip planning less overwhelming?

  • 12needfinding sessions
  • 3lo-fi prototypes
  • 7think-aloud evals
  • 20person comparative study
THE PROBLEM //

Study-abroad students burn hours planning trips, juggling group interests, budgets, and time constraints, usually ending in compromise fatigue.

DISCOVERY //

Chose methods deliberately: sat in on real group planning sessions (participant observation) and ran 1:1 interviews, rejecting surveys and focus groups for lack of depth. Key insights: students average $20–50 per activity, spend multiple sessions planning, and transportation logistics were the recurring pain.

THE BET //

Prioritized a swipeable discovery feed filtered by budget, interest, and location, betting that a familiar TikTok-style mental model would flatten the learning curve. Explored three lo-fi directions before committing to a high-fidelity Figma prototype.

VALIDATION //

Ran 7 moderated think-alouds plus a 20-person between-subjects study (lo-fi control vs hi-fi, t-test). The hi-fi version did not significantly raise satisfaction (p = 0.60), instead it surfaced discoverability, affordance, and hierarchy issues that polish alone couldn't fix.

WHAT I LEARNED //

Translated findings into a concrete next iteration: simplified homepage hierarchy, a first-use swipe affordance, clearer navigation, and preference-capturing onboarding. Biggest lesson: a familiar pattern only works with clear affordances, and a null result is still a roadmap.
